max(X, Y) or sqrt(X)?

January 3, 2025

Intro

I originally learnt this from Stand-up Maths who creates excellent entertaining videos about fun results like this one. Suppose you have a random number generator that returns numbers between 0 and 1 with equal probability. Would you rather pick the maximum of two random numbers or the (positive) square root of one random number if you want the largest value?

In more formal mathematical terms, let XXX and YYY be two independent random variables with the standard uniform distribution.

X,Y∼U(0,1)X, Y \sim U(0, 1)X,Y∼U(0,1)

First observations

What do max(X,Y)max(X, Y)max(X,Y) and sqrt(X)sqrt(X)sqrt(X) do at a basic level? Imagine XXX as a random point on a line between 0 and 1.

Given a particular value of XXX, max(X,Y)max(X, Y)max(X,Y) must be greater than or equal to XXX by definition.

Similarly, defining sqrt(X)sqrt(X)sqrt(X) as the unique positive square root must be greater than or equal to XXX due to the value of XXX being in the interval [0,1][0, 1][0,1]. For example, 0.25=0.5\sqrt{0.25}=0.50.25​=0.5, 0.5≈0.707\sqrt{0.5} \approx 0.7070.5​≈0.707.

Transforming the problem

We can transform the problem to view the intuition behind this visually. As we have two random independent variables, rather than a random point on a 1D line imagine XXX and YYY as a random point on a 2D grid where each point is equally likely to be picked.

Conclusion

The result is hard to believe at first. How could max(X,Y)max(X, Y)max(X,Y) and sqrt(X)sqrt(X)sqrt(X) possibly be identically distributed? With this magic setup these two random functions can in fact be linked together where introducing the second independent identically distributed random variable YYY to XXX has an effect of introducing "squaring" to the problem.

We also saw that transforming the problem to visualise the problem in a new way can help provide intuition into how a structure behaves and aid constructing a proof. This powerful technique pops up often in mathematics and can remarkably link one problem to another, sometimes in a completely different branch of mathematics!
